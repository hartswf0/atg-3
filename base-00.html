OPERATIVE EKPHRASIS: The WAG Protocols and the Democratization of Spatial Computing

An Open-Source Research Brief Documenting the Words Assemble Geometry Initiative

1. Executive Summary: The Crisis of Translation

Abstract. The contemporary landscape of spatial design is governed by a technical gatekeeping regime that privileges engineering proficiency over imaginative vision. The Words Assemble Geometry (WAG) initiative proposes a paradigm shift from manual construction to semantic compilation, where natural language prompts generate navigable 3D worlds through the mechanism of Operative Ekphrasis. This report documents an open-source ecosystem—including planning engines, recursive IDEs, and AI agents—designed to democratize spatial computing for designers, researchers, and storytellers who lack traditional coding expertise. Through case studies of five co-designers spanning speculative futurism to therapeutic VR, we demonstrate that removing technical barriers enables culturally-specific, socially-impactful world-building that centralized engineering cannot replicate.

1.1 The Trap of Technicality

In spatial design and digital world-building, a profound chasm exists between vision and realization. Creators construct elaborate narrative worlds within the "theatre of the mind," yet translation into 3D reality is strictly policed by technical expertise. The current paradigm demands mastery of complex coding languages, game engines, and prohibitively expensive software—creating a filter that selects for technical proficiency rather than compelling vision. The result is homogenized digital space, where aesthetics are dictated by engineering limitations rather than human imagination.[1]

1.2 The WAG Proposition: Words as Compilers

WAG proposes a radical ontological shift: from manual construction to semantic compilation. The core claim—"Words don't describe worlds, they compile them"—reframes natural language as performative code. The "Wild Ass Guess" (WAG) heuristic leverages linguistic ambiguity as a feature for rapid prototyping, turning Freirean pedagogy into technical practice.[2]

2. Theoretical Framework: Operative Ekphrasis

2.1 Classical vs. Operative Ekphrasis

Classical ekphrasis describes the literary representation of visual art—text describing an existing image. Operative Ekphrasis, by contrast, collapses this distinction: the text generates the image by executing within the latent space of multimodal AI.[3] In WAG, prompts become performative code.

Feature Classical Ekphrasis Operative Ekphrasis
Primary Function Representation / Description Generation / Compilation
Relationship Text describes existing Image Text causes new Image
Mediums Distinct (Text vs. Visual) Collapsed (Latent Data)
Role of Code None Text acts as performative code

Table 1: Comparative analysis of ekphrastic modalities in spatial computing.

2.2 The Feedback Loop: From Prompt to World

// WAG COMPILATION PIPELINE ───────────────────────────────────── Input: User provides narrative vision ("Build a cabin with a porch") Operation: AI parses semantic intent → latent space traversal → geometry instantiation Output: 3D model manifests in viewport Iteration: User refines via subsequent prompts ("Add rusted metal roof", "Make it stoic")

This recursive loop transforms users from consumers into active shapers, fulfilling the Freirean promise of technology as liberation.[4]

WAG's efficacy is validated through a participatory research model where community members function as co-authors rather than end-users. Each collaborator embodies a distinct use-case, stress-testing the ecosystem against real-world design challenges.

WAG Integration: Caldwell's work represents pure operative ekphrasis. Her "Training Grounds" workbook serves as a narrative architecture that WAG translates into navigable simulations without requiring code. She builds "future worlds" from cultural archival material, proving that deep complexity emerges from semantic depth, not technical sophistication.

5.2 Thousand-Tetrad: A Pluriverse of Futures

The "Thousand-Tetrad" concept rejects the monolithic corporate Metaverse in favor of a thousand small futures—culturally-specific, socially-impactful, and authored by those with lived experience. Each co-designer embodies a distinct future:

  • Caldwell's Future: Afrofuturist space exploration grounded in spiritual cosmology
  • Evans's Future: Warm, conversational architectures that privilege social equity
  • Tramontini's Future: Ecological simulations testing human-nature co-evolution
  • Marttila's Future: Migratory narratives as spatial poems of displacement
  • Jang's Future: Neurodivergent-first therapeutic spaces for skill development

These futures are distinct, specific, and culturally rich. They cannot be built by centralized engineering teams lacking the creators' lived experience. They can only emerge when the builders themselves hold the tools.

6. Conclusion: Permission Structures

WAG is not fundamentally about 3D modeling; it is about permission. In the current regime, permission to build the future is granted by computer science degrees or capital investment. WAG constructs a new permission structure where the word itself becomes the credential.

By proving that "Words don't describe worlds, they compile them," WAG hands the compiler to the storyteller. It validates the Wild Ass Guess as legitimate design methodology. The ecosystem—from ONYX Studio's planning logic to CORE-AGE's recursive power to the Artifact Series' cultural anchoring—is designed to support this transfer of power.

The co-designer community serves as proof-of-concept: removing technical gates does not produce chaos but Operative Ekphrasis. It yields a "Carrot Uprising" of organic, diverse, human-centric creativity that transforms the rigid digital grid into a canvas for liberation.

Final Proposition

The answer to "Can we prompt scenarios without technical expertise?" is Yes—but only if we treat the word as the ultimate architectural unit and the storyteller as the ultimate architect.
