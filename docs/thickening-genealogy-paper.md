# Thickening as Infrastructure: A Political Economy of Viscosity from Kitchen Pharmacology to Clinical Protocol

## Abstract

This paper presents a **genealogy of thickening** as infrastructural intervention. We argue that thickeners are not merely ingredients but rather *technologies of control*—ways to make liquids behave predictably across distance, time, regulatory regimes, and industrial machinery. Drawing on infrastructure studies (Star, Bowker, Latour) and deploying an **Inverse Generative Social Science (IGSS)** lens, we trace how viscosity manipulation migrates from embodied craft knowledge to extractable commodities, from local availability to global supply-chain dependencies, and from kitchen technique to medical protocol. The resulting framework allows analysts to "read backwards" from any thickener on a label to the institutional constraints it was invented to solve.

**Keywords:** infrastructure studies, viscosity, food systems, political economy, supply chains, standardization, inverse generative social science

---

## 1. Introduction: Thickeners Beyond Ingredients

When you encounter xanthan gum in salad dressing, carrageenan in plant milk, or a "thickened liquid" on a hospital menu, you are not simply encountering an additive. You are encountering a *solution to an infrastructural problem*—one that typically involves some combination of:

- **Distance** (the sauce must survive shipping)
- **Time** (the product must remain stable on shelves)
- **Regulation** (the consistency must meet specifications)
- **Machinery** (the fluid must flow through pumps and nozzles predictably)

This paper develops a **genealogy of thickening** that treats viscosity not as a property of matter, but as a *designed relationship* between liquids and the institutions that need them to behave.

We draw on infrastructure studies, particularly the work of Susan Leigh Star and Geoffrey Bowker on classification, Bruno Latour on inscription and immutable mobiles, and James C. Scott on legibility. Our analytic method is **Inverse Generative Social Science (IGSS)**: rather than asking "what does this thickener do?" we ask "what institutional constraints *generated the need* for this thickener?"

## 2. Pre-Industrial Thickening: Kitchen Pharmacology (Before 1800)

For most of human history, thickening belongs to what we might call **kitchen pharmacology**: the use of locally available whole materials through embodied technique.

### 2.1 Materials

The pre-industrial thickener toolkit includes:

| Material | Source | Mechanism |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| **Flour/Starch** | Grains, tubers | Gelatinization on heating |
| **Eggs** | Poultry | Protein coagulation |
| **Collagen/Gelatin** | Animal bones, skin | Thermal gelation |
| **Plant mucilages** | Flax, chia, mallow | Hydrophilic polymer swelling |
| **Seaweeds** | Coastal harvesting | Polysaccharide extraction |

### 2.2 The Logic of Local Availability + Embodied Practice

The key point is not technical innovation—these materials have been used for millennia—but rather **local availability and embodied practice**. Thickening is achieved through:

- Reduction (simmering to concentrate)
- Tempering (adding eggs without curdling)
- Cooling (allowing gelatin to set)
- Whipping (incorporating air for texture)

This knowledge is tacit, situated, and transmitted through apprenticeship. In Latour's terms, it is **not inscribed**—it does not travel without the practitioner.

### 2.3 The Medical Parallel

In pre-industrial medicine, "thick" often means *therapeutic*:

- **Syrups**: thick consistency carries active ingredients, coats the throat
- **Poultices**: thick pastes stay in place on wounds
- **Mucilages**: soothing, coating, slow-release

Viscosity in medicine implies *staying put* and *controlled release*—properties that will become formalized in later pharmaceutical design.

## 3. The Industrial Revolution Learns Viscosity (1800s)

The 19th century marks the migration of thickening from craft to **chemistry** and **commodity supply chain**.

### 3.1 Extraction and Standardization

The key transformation is **extraction**: isolating the functional component from the whole material.

**1825: Pectin** is chemically isolated and named by Henri Braconnot. What was previously jam-making know-how—certain fruits gel better than others, cooking time matters—becomes an **extractable, tradable functional ingredient**. Pectin is no longer a property of apples; it is a substance that can be manufactured, standardized, and sold.

### 3.2 Thickening as Scientific Instrument

Microbiology creates new demands for thickening as **measurement surface**.

- **Gelatin** becomes the first common solidifying agent for culture media in the Koch era (1880s)
- **Agar** quickly replaces gelatin because it remains solid at incubation temperatures (37°C)

**IGSS Reading**: Once laboratories need *reproducible surfaces* at *specific temperatures*, thickening is no longer about taste—it is about **control**. The agar plate is an infrastructure for industrial science.

### 3.3 From Embodied Practice to Commodity Logic

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| "This fruit makes jam set well" | "This product contains standardized pectin" |
| "My grandmother's bone broth gels overnight" | "Add gelatin powder, measured in grams" |
| Knowledge travels with people | Knowledge travels with products |

The thickener becomes an **immutable mobile**: it can be shipped, stored, and used without local knowledge (Latour, 1987).

## 4. Agar, Empire, and Supply Shocks (Late 1800s–WWII)

Agar is the exemplary case of a **thickener becoming strategic material**.

### 4.1 Origins and Adoption

- Culinary roots in East Asia (Japanese *kanten*, Malay *agar-agar*)
- Adopted in microbiology after 1880s (Walther Hesse, via his wife Fanny Hesse)
- Becomes the **standard** solidifying agent for bacterial culture

### 4.2 Concentration and Crisis

As microbiology scales, agar supply concentrates—notably in Japan, which controls production and export.

**World War II** exposes the vulnerability: Allied nations need agar for military medical and food applications, but supply from Japan is cut off.

Result: **forced substitution industries**:
- Development of alternative agar sources (other seaweed species)
- Research into local seaweed harvesting in the U.S. and Europe
- Post-war diversification of production (FAO programs)

### 4.3 IGSS Reading

When you see a thickener become "strategic," you are seeing **institutions requiring stable materials** and then **states or industries reorganizing supply**.

Agar's trajectory follows the infrastructure studies template:
1. Local/artisanal use → laboratory adoption → industrial scale
2. Supply concentration → dependency → crisis
3. State intervention → diversification → new global supply chains

The thickener is never just technical; it is **geopolitical**.

## 5. The Processed-Food Boom Invents Texture Engineering (1930–1965)

A cluster of carbohydrate thickeners becomes industrially important in a tight 30-year window. This is the **texture engineering revolution**.

### 5.1 The Hydrocolloid Explosion

Drawing on BeMiller's (2009) "One hundred years of commercial food carbohydrates":

| Thickener | Industrial Emergence |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **Carrageenan** | Industry begins 1930s, grows rapidly 1940s–50s |
| **Alginate** | Expands in 1930s–40s |
| **Guar gum** | Industry develops 1940s–50s |
| **Methylcellulose/CMC** | Production begins 1940s |
| **Xanthan gum** | Emerges 1950s–60s |
| **Modified starches** | Widespread by 1950s |

### 5.2 What Problems Are These Solving?

Mass food systems need:

| Problem | Thickener Solution |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Shelf stability** | Polysaccharides prevent syneresis (water separation) |
| **Freeze-thaw stability** | Modified starches resist retrogradation |
| **Cheap mouthfeel** | Gums replace expensive fats and eggs |
| **Machine compatibility** | Controlled viscosity for pumping, filling, spraying |

### 5.3 The Political Economy Punchline

> Thickeners help capitalism sell **consistency**—the same sauce, the same yogurt, the same "creamy" experience—no matter the season, factory location, or shipping distance.

Standardization through viscosity is **legibility** (Scott, 1998): the raw materials become readable, controllable, interchangeable. What is lost is the local, the seasonal, the artisanal variation. What is gained is scale.

## 6. Fermentation Thickeners and the State-Industry Lab Pipeline (1950s–1970s)

### 6.1 Xanthan Gum: From Public Research to Platform Ingredient

**Discovery**: 1950s, USDA Northern Regional Research Laboratory (Peoria, Illinois)

**Researcher**: Allene Rosalind Jeanes and collaborators

**Commercial production**: 1964 (Kelco Company)

Xanthan spreads across:
- Food (dressings, sauces, gluten-free baking)
- Pharmaceuticals (suspensions, drug delivery)
- Oil drilling (drilling mud viscosifier)
- Cosmetics, personal care

**Why xanthan works**: Powerful thickening at low concentrations; stable across pH, temperature, and shear.

**IGSS Reading**: Xanthan demonstrates the **public research → industrial platform** pipeline. USDA research serves agricultural commodity interests (corn syrup fermentation), generates IP that transfers to private industry, and creates an ingredient that becomes infrastructural across sectors.

### 6.2 Dextran: Thickening Blood

At the same Northern Regional Research Lab, Allene Jeanes develops **dextran**—a bacterial polysaccharide that becomes a **blood volume expander**.

- Used by U.S. Army during Korean War context
- Enables trauma resuscitation without refrigeration
- Dextran is "thickened blood substitute"—viscosity matters for circulatory function

**IGSS Reading**: In emergency medicine, thickening is not mouthfeel—it is **circulatory survivability under logistics constraints**. Dextran solves: storage (no refrigeration), transport (stable), and immediate use (simply add saline).

### 6.3 Hydroxyethyl Starch (HES)

**1960s**: Another starch-derivative colloid enters clinical use

**Later**: Becomes controversial (associated with kidney injury, mortality in certain populations)

The HES case illustrates that thickening in medicine is never neutral—it carries risks that become visible only at scale, over decades.

## 7. Thickening as Protocol and Classification Warfare (Late 20th Century → Now)

### 7.1 Thickened Liquids for Dysphagia

Thickeners become part of **routine clinical care**: patients with swallowing difficulties receive thickened fluids.

**The protocol**:
- Thin liquids → aspiration risk
- Thickened liquids → slowed bolus transit, reduced aspiration
- Starch-based and gum-based thickeners used

**Tradeoffs**:
- Reduced hydration (patients drink less when liquids are thick)
- Changed drug absorption (thickeners may affect bioavailability)
- Quality of life considerations

**IGSS Reading**: Clinical thickening is **protocolization**—the transformation of messy embodied consumption (swallowing) into something manufacturable, measurable, and governable. Viscosity becomes a prescription.

### 7.2 Carrageenan/Poligeenan: Classification Warfare

A major contemporary politics-of-knowledge fight:

**The distinction**:
- **Carrageenan** (food-grade): high molecular weight, used as thickener/stabilizer
- **Poligeenan** (degraded carrageenan): low molecular weight, not food-grade

**The controversy**:
- IARC classifies **degraded carrageenan (poligeenan)** as Group 2B (possibly carcinogenic)
- Industry argues food-grade carrageenan is distinct and safe
- Advocacy groups exploit terminological ambiguity

**IGSS Reading**: Once ingredients live on labels, thickening becomes **classification warfare**:

| Actor | Strategy |
|-------|----------|
| Industry | Stabilize categories: "food-grade is not degraded" |
| Advocates | Exploit ambiguity: "carrageenan causes cancer" |
| Regulators | Arbitrate what counts as "same substance" under law |

This is classification as described by Bowker and Star (1999): the work of making and maintaining categories is political, contested, and consequential.

## 8. The IGSS Model: Reading the Label Backward

**Inverse Generative Social Science** asks: given an observed outcome (a thickener on a label), what generative constraints produced it?

### 8.1 The Four Generative Constraints

| Constraint | What to look for | Typical thickener response |
|------------|------------------|---------------------------|
| **Distance + Time** | Shelf stability, freeze-thaw, heat/acid stability, shear resistance | Hydrocolloids, modified starches |
| **Cost** | Cheap solids, cheap creaminess, cheap suspension | Gums replace fat, eggs, reduction |
| **Standardization** | "Tastes the same everywhere" | Thickeners as quality control infrastructure |
| **Clinical Governance** | Dosage uniformity, swallow safety, controlled release | Medicalized viscosity, protocol |

### 8.2 Worked Example: Plant-Based Milk

Pick up oat milk. Read the label. You may find:
- **Gellan gum**: suspension (oats sink without it)
- **Sunflower lecithin**: emulsification
- **Dipotassium phosphate**: buffer, calcium stability

**Reverse inference**:
- This product must survive shipping (suspension)
- This product must seem "creamy" without dairy fat (mouthfeel)
- This product must be consistent batch-to-batch (brand promise)
- This product must work in coffee (heat stability, no curdling)

The thickeners are compensation for **what oats + water don't naturally do**—behave like milk across the supply chain.

### 8.3 Worked Example: Medical Nutrition Shake

Read the label on Ensure, Boost, or similar:
- **Cellulose gel, cellulose gum**: suspension, mouthfeel
- **Carrageenan**: stabilization
- **Sucralose, flavors**: palatability

**Reverse inference**:
- This product must deliver measured nutrients (not variable food)
- This product must be drinkable by patients with compromised swallowing
- This product must survive hospital logistics (shelf life, no refrigeration)
- This product must taste acceptable despite being functional

The thickeners are compensation for **nutritional medicine's requirements**: precision, stability, palatability.

---

## 9. Conclusions: Infrastructure All the Way Down

Thickeners are infrastructure. They are:

- **Invisible** when working (you don't notice viscosity unless it fails)
- **Embedded** in larger systems (supply chains, clinical protocols, quality control)
- **Dependent on installed base** (once industry uses guar gum, formulations assume guar gum)
- **Subject to politics** (classification, regulation, safety debates)

This paper has traced thickening from kitchen pharmacology to clinical protocol, showing how each era's thickener repertoire reveals the institutional constraints of its time.

The IGSS method allows us to read any thickener as a symptom—not of taste preference, but of the logistical, economic, regulatory, and clinical apparatus that required it.
